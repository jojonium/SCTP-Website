History of the Central Police Station

One of the first things built by the British when they took control of Hong Kong in 1841 was the Central Police Station and Victoria Prison. This compound was used for many purposes throughout the end of the 19th century and most of the 20th century. Throughout the 1900s there were many renovations of the prison, expanding it. After World War II it turned into an Immigration Department office and was processing many illegal immigrants and refugees from Vietnam around the 1980s.

But by 2006 the prison and the police station were turned into a historic site and were no longer being used for their original purposes. Today the compound that consists of the Central Police Station, Victoria Prison and the Former Central Magistracy is now restored and parts have been turned into an arts complex. The restoration took a total of eight years and includes all the cells in Victoria Prison, signs and brick work on the outside of the building and many rooms within the buildings, all meant to show what the buildings originally looked like. The new “art complex” includes a mix of contemporary and modern styles made from concrete and glass that stand out from the old brickwork of the original prison and police station.

Modern Preservation Efforts

The Central Police Station (CPS) Compound has been renovated and put into reuse, as a newly developed complex referred to as Tai Kwun. Although the CPS compound was officially announced as an historic monument in 1995, the great economic potential brought by its superior location in the Central District caused conflicts between the local residents and developers in 2003. In order to boost Hong Kong’s economy in 2001, the Chief Executive of Hong Kong, Tung Chee-hwa, changed the CPS compound from the public-community-used zone into the specific uses zone. It was marked as a “historical building preserved for cultural, community, and commercial uses”. The Hong Kong government announced the plan for private tender of the CPS compound in April 2003. As public concern for heritage preservation rose, the government’s plan was criticized by many local people. The plan was shelved in 2004 when a group of wealthy people, the Hotung Group, proposed a different plan, which focused more on preserving culture rather than making profit. They received increasing attention and support from locals and the press when they proposed a HK$500 million donation for this proposal. From 2003 to 2007 a heritage preservation group was formed, and surveys of public opinion were conducted under the goal of preserving historic sites.

In April 2007 the Hong Kong Jockey Club (HKJC) proposed a revitalization plan and a donation to the government. This proposal was accepted by the government and announced in the 2007 Policy Address. The government became the partner of HKJC after three months, and they worked together to promote the preservation plan. Public opinion and designs for the buildings were collected over the next six months. In October 2010 the revised design of the CPS compound project was announced, and construction started in 2011 and continued for five years.Today the Tai Kwun is reopened with sixteen restored buildings after eleven years of work done by HKJC and visited by both locals and tourists.

Location

Tai Kwun is located in the Central district of Hong Kong Island, on Hollywood road. There are five public entrances:

  1. Bauhinia House Gate
  2. Blue Gate
  3. Footbridge Gate
  4. Old Bailey Gate
  5. Pottinger Gate

Tai Kwun is connected to the Central–Mid-Levels escalator through a footbridge at the intersection of Hollywood Road and Old Bailey Street. The compound is typically open from 10:00 AM to 11:00 PM. Information about the hours of specific exhibitions can be found at the Tai Kwun website.
