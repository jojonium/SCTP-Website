History of the Central Market

Located on Queen's Road Central, the Central Market is one of the oldest markets in Hong Kong, providing residents with everything from fresh food to daily essentials. The precursor of the Central Market was the Canton Bazaar, which opened in 1842 and moved to the current location of the market in the 1850s. The market was rebuilt in 1859 into a Victorian house with three stories, but was later demolished in 1937. A new market was constructed on the site in 1939 as a Bauhaus structure with four stories and 263 stalls in total. It was once the largest meat market in Southeast Asia, and was listed as a Grade III Historic Building in 1990.

Modern Preservation Efforts

After the completion of the Mid-Levels Escalator in 1994, the upper levels of the Central Market were transformed into a shopping arcade. However, by 2000 the government had considered the market a waste of land due to its low usage, and it was closed in 2003. The Urban Renewal Authority announced a new preservation plan in the Chif Executive's Policy Address in 2007, and conducted community surveys and investigations on future uses of the Central Market between 2009 and 2011. The final design plan was proposed in March 2016 and approved by the Town Planning Board after one year. The proposal includes 1,000 square meters of open space for the public and 12,000 square meters for retail facilities. In 2017 the government handed the Central Market to the URA, allowing private operation and revitalization for the next 21 years. The construction is still in progress and is estimated to be completed by 2022.
